Computer Science as Discipline, Computing Disciplines and Majors

Computer Science as Discipline

Foundations in Mathematics and Engineering

Computer science is fundamentally rooted in mathematics and engineering, with a primary focus on algorithms, programming, and hardware design as the core activities (Denning et al., 1989).

Misconception of Computer Science as Programming

There is a prevailing view that equates computer science solely with programming, which is reflected in many current curricula. This narrow focus on programming, especially in introductory courses, limits the development of a more comprehensive curriculum and discourages top students seeking greater challenges (Denning et al., 1989).

Broad Scope Beyond Programming

Computer science encompasses far more than programming. It includes hardware design, system architecture, operating system layers, database structuring, and model validation. These aspects are integral to the discipline but are often overshadowed by the emphasis on programming (Denning et al., 1989).

Potential of Computer Science in Cognitive Sciences

If fully explored, computer science has the potential to elevate our understanding of intellectual processes, including learning, thinking, and reasoning. It offers models and conceptual tools that can contribute significantly to the cognitive sciences (Hopcroft, 1987).

Debate on the Nature of Computer Science

The discipline of computer science lacks clear boundaries and is sometimes debated regarding its exact nature and even its name. Edsger Dijkstra famously stated, "Computer science is no more about computers than astronomy is about telescopes," suggesting that the field is more about understanding processes and behavior rather than the physical machines themselves (Siekmann, 2014).

Postmodern Characteristics of Computer Science

Unlike traditional sciences like chemistry or physics, computer science has been described as a "postmodern" science, where success is often measured by commercial success and public recognition rather than peer-reviewed publications. This has led to rapid advancements, sometimes at the expense of deeper academic reflection (Akera & Aspray, 2005).

Young Discipline with Rapid Growth

Computer science is a relatively young field, emerging in the 1940s with the creation of the ENIAC computer and the stored-program paradigm. The discipline only gained distinct academic recognition in the 1960s, but its rapid evolution has made it difficult to define its exact scope and methods (Tedre, 2007).

Historical Context and Evolution

The history of computer science is marked by significant milestones, such as the development of the ENIAC and the adoption of the stored-program paradigm. These milestones have shaped the field's evolution, but also highlight the rapid and sometimes contentious growth of the discipline (Tedre, 2007).

Interdisciplinary Challenges

The development of computer science has been significantly influenced by interdisciplinarity, drawing from fields such as mathematics, electrical engineering, and psychology. However, this also poses challenges, as it is difficult to delineate what should be considered "computer science proper" and to establish a unified methodology for research in the field (Tedre, 2007).

5 Computing Disciplines and Majors

Computer Engineering

Interdisciplinary Nature

Computer engineering merges principles from electrical engineering, mathematics, and computer science to design and build hardware and software systems. This field has significantly influenced student academic achievement, particularly through innovations like online learning (Al Hussaini, 2023).

Role in Education

The historical evolution of computer engineering, with its focus on creating reliable and efficient systems, has made it integral to the development of both educational technologies and curricula (Al Hussaini, 2023).

Computer Science

Mathematically-Based Analysis

Computer science focuses on technical levels of Analysis, using mathematically-based processes, methods, and algorithms. Unlike other computing disciplines, it does not heavily rely on external reference disciplines (Glass, Ramesh, & Vessey, 2004).

Core Discipline Identity

The discipline examines fundamental concepts such as algorithms and computational theory, forming a solid core that distinguishes it from other fields, like software engineering, which may employ less mathematically-intensive approaches (Glass et al., 2004).

Information Systems

Behavioral and Organizational Focus

Information Systems (IS) differs significantly from Computer Science and Software Engineering by focusing on organizational concepts and the behavioral Analysis of technology usage. It draws from a variety of reference disciplines, especially from business schools (Glass et al., 2004).

Crisis and Identity

The IS discipline is currently facing challenges in defining its theoretical foundation and core identity, which has led to debates about its legitimacy and relevance within academia and industry. This lack of a unified core has both hindered and diversified the discipline's research output (Bakshi & Krishna, 2007).

Information Technology

Evolution of the Discipline

Information Technology (IT) has evolved from industry-driven roots to become a recognized academic discipline. The timeline of its development reflects phases from the advent of IT in the 1980s to its current status as a crucial area of study, although pinpointing the exact moment of its independence remains challenging (Basty, Celik, & Said, 2023).

Industry-Academia Connection

IT's growth in academia mirrors the industry's increasing need for a well-prepared workforce, highlighting its importance in both educational and professional contexts (Basty et al., 2023).

Software Engineering

Emergence and Academic Recognition

Software Engineering (SE) emerged as a distinct discipline in the mid-1980s, with academic programs first appearing at institutions like Carnegie Mellon University and Imperial College London. It focuses on the research, design, construction, and management of software systems, incorporating principles from mathematics, information science, and computer science (Yeboah et al., 2022).

Human-Centric and Ethical Concerns

IT's growth in academia mirrors the industry's increasing need for a well-prepared workforce, highlighting its importance in both educational and professional contexts (Basty et al., 2023).

Analysis

Computer science has undergone significant changes since its early days in the 1940s. Traditionally, it was closely tied to mathematics and engineering, and there has been a strong focus on programming as the core of the discipline. However, this view is increasingly seen as too narrow. Computer science actually encompasses a wide range of activities beyond just programming, including hardware design, system architecture, database management, and theoretical modeling. This broader perspective is important because it reflects the true diversity of the field and can make the discipline more appealing to students like me who seek a challenging and comprehensive education.

There are various limitations of focusing solely on programming. This view restricts our understanding of what computer science involves. By emphasizing programming to the exclusion of other areas, we miss out on the full range of topics that computer science covers. For instance, understanding how to design and structure hardware, develop operating systems, or validate models are all crucial aspects of computer science that extend beyond coding. In addition to practical applications, computer science also offers insights into intellectual processes and cognitive science. Computer science is not just about computing devices but about understanding complex processes such as thinking, learning, and reasoning. This perspective shows that computer science contributes to a deeper understanding of how we process information and make decisions, which can be valuable in various academic and practical contexts.

One of the challenges in defining computer science is its interdisciplinary nature. The field integrates concepts from mathematics, engineering, and logic, which can make it hard to pin down a single definition. This rapid evolution and blending with other disciplines complicates the task of defining what computer science is and how research should be conducted. While this interdisciplinarity fosters innovation and growth, it also makes it difficult to establish a cohesive identity for the field.

When compared to related disciplines like software engineering (SE) and information systems (IS), computer science has distinct characteristics. Software engineering focuses on the development, design, and management of software systems. It deals with both technical aspects, such as algorithms and system design, and human-centered issues, such as team dynamics and software quality. On the other hand, information systems concentrate on organizational aspects and technology transfer, using methods like field studies and case studies to explore how technology impacts organizations.

In summary, computer science is a diverse and evolving field that includes much more than just programming. It involves a wide range of activities, from hardware design to theoretical modeling, and contributes to our understanding of cognitive processes. The challenge for the discipline is to articulate a clear identity while addressing its many aspects and integrating with other fields. As computer science continues to grow and evolve, finding a balance between its various components will be crucial for its development and its role in academia and industry.
